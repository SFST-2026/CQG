Abstract


I present a reproducibility-focused study of vacuum-induced inertial corrections in neutron-star structure, combining an effective-field-theory matching procedure with controlled numerical solutions of the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff equations. Methods: I fix kernel and unit conventions (CODATA SI↔geometrized units, C_conv=1), implement two insertion hypotheses (Variant A: inertial-only, default; Variant B: inertial + gravitational source, sensitivity check), and quantify regulator-scheme dependence via parallel Pauli–Villars and dimensional-regularization scans. All numerical runs are archived in a frozen repository snapshot (code, deterministic seeds, Dockerfile, CI workflows, and SHA256SUMS) so reviewers can reproduce canonical cases and sensitivity scans without external dependencies. Numerical controls: I enforce single-threaded BLAS, explicit RNG seeding, and a documented grid-refinement ladder with Richardson extrapolation, Newton residual traces, and WFaktor stability diagnostics; acceptance/exclusion rules for non-convergent runs are applied and recorded. Uncertainty quantification: regulator variation is propagated using measured finite-difference sensitivities S_O across multiple \(\Delta\sigma\) spans; where responses are strongly nonlinear (notably \(\Lambda_{1.4}\)), I report quadratic-fit and Monte-Carlo propagated bands and adopt conservative quadrature combination δO_total = sqrt(δO_disc^2 + δO_match^2 + δO_stat^2). Results (summary): across a representative set of realistic EOS (SLy, AP4, MPA1, H4, WFF1) Variant A produces systematic but modest shifts in maximum mass and radii; tidal deformabilities show larger sensitivity and require the full regulator-envelope treatment reported here. Cases with extreme discretization uncertainty (e.g., δ_total > 50% for \(\Lambda_{1.4}\)) are treated as diagnostic-only until refinement and convergence diagnostics demonstrate stability. Availability: the repository snapshot and generated outputs (CSV, plots, logs) accompany this submission; a one-command reproducibility entrypoint and a short smoke test validate the canonical outputs. All substantive numerical claims in the main text reference the archived artefacts; readers may rescale results to alternative kernel normalizations using the supplied rescaling scripts.

A2. SI → geometrized units (c=G=1): transparent derivation + round-trip check

In geometrized units, mass is expressed as a length via:

[M]_geom = (G/c^2) [M]_SI

Time is expressed as a length via:

[t]_geom = c [t]_SI

Energy satisfies E = M c^2, hence:

[E]_geom = (G/c^4) [E]_SI

Using CODATA values for G and c, numeric conversion factors follow directly. To prevent hidden factor errors, I recommend a SI→geom→SI round-trip check to machine precision:


```python

import math

G = 6.67430e-11 # m^3 kg^-1 s^-2

c = 299792458.0 # m s^-1

kg_to_m = G/(c**2)

J_to_m = G/(c**4)

xJ = 1.234567

xJ_geom = xJ * J_to_m

xJ_back = xJ_geom / J_to_m

assert math.isclose(xJ_back, xJ, rel_tol=0.0, abs_tol=1e-15)

```

A3b. Physical interpretation of σ and χ (plausibility bounds and rescaling)


Physical meaning in this addendum. In my implementation the combination σχ is the effective low-frequency coefficient that enters the numerical model (m_vac ≡ σχ under my fixed conventions). I treat χ=1 as a transparent stand-in that makes σ itself the single dial of the scan; any alternative choice χ≠1 is a pure reparameterization and can be absorbed by σ→σ/χ (with C_conv fixed to 1). Plausibility guidance. I choose σ spans by (i) matching-envelope size from the regulator scan, (ii) solver stability (Newton residuals, CFL checks), and (iii) diagnostic plausibility (WFaktor_max). I explicitly do not interpret the largest σ values as physically preferred; they are stress tests of numerical and modelling robustness. Rescaling example. If a reader adopts a different χ or kernel normalization α (K→αK), the mapping becomes σ_new = (α/χ_new) σ_ref, and all reported ΔO(σ) curves can be reused by simple axis rescaling; I provide a script in the snapshot that performs this rescaling and regenerates the tables.

C_conv: operational definition (removes σ-rescaling ambiguity)

I define the effective action for the slow coordinate x(t) as:

S_eff[x] = (1/2) ∫ (dω/2π) x(-ω) K(ω) x(ω)

and expand the reactive retarded kernel as:

K_R(ω) = K_R(0) + (1/2) m_vac ω^2 + O(ω^4).

With these conventions, the induced inertial parameter is by definition:

m_vac ≡ (d^2 K_R / dω^2)|_{ω=0}

so the matching coefficient satisfies C_conv = 1 in this manuscript. Any alternative kernel normalization changes C_conv accordingly; I fix the above normalization globally to avoid ambiguity.

A11.0 Outlier policy, diagnostics, and nonlinearity checks (reviewer-facing)


How I treat pathological cases (high δdisc, extreme Λ or W_max)

A few EOS/cases can show **extreme discretization error estimates** (e.g., δdisc ≫ 100%) or numerically suspicious derived quantities (very large Λ or W_max). I treat these as **pathological diagnostics**, not as headline evidence:


- **Headline tables/claims** exclude any run that fails my acceptance criteria (grid-ladder convergence, solver residual thresholds, and WFaktor sanity bounds).

- **Supplement-only reporting**: I keep the full rows for transparency, but I flag them as *diagnostic/outlier* and link the per-case diagnostics folder.

- **Per-case explanation**: for every flagged row I include a short `diagnosis.txt` that states whether the driver is (i) grid underresolution, (ii) EOS-specific stiffness/instability, (iii) parameter-scan regime outside the validated linear-response window, or (iv) a confirmed bug/formatting issue (with a pointer to the fixing commit).


Concretely, the most visible outliers in earlier drafts (e.g., **WFF1 Λ_1.4 with δdisc ~ O(10^2–10^3)%** and the **Poly2(toy)** extremes) are treated as **non-headline** until extended refinement or additional diagnostics demonstrate stable convergence. In the snapshot, I provide the automation and folder structure needed to attach the complete diagnostics for these runs (see `outputs/diagnostics/` and `supplement/diagnostics_index.md`).


Diagnostics I attach for each flagged run

For every run with δdisc or δtotal above a declared threshold (default: 100%) **or** WFaktor_max above the “flag” threshold, I attach:


- Richardson extrapolation / observed order plot

- Newton residual trace (iteration history)

- WFaktor(r) profile + WFaktor_max summary

- run log with solver tolerances, grid size, and determinism settings


These artefacts live under `outputs/diagnostics/<EOS>_<case>/` and are indexed from the Supplement.


**Audit trail:** For every run I label DIAGNOSTIC or EXCLUDED, I also write an entry to `outputs/audit_trail.csv` (EOS, case tag, triggered rule(s), and paths to logs/plots).


Nonlinearity checks for large responses (especially Λ)

Nonlinearity reporting requirement. When \(\Lambda_{1.4}\) (or any other observable) shows >20% spread in \(S_O\) across \(\Delta\sigma\) spans, I do not report a single linear sensitivity. Instead I archive: (i) \(\Delta O\) vs \(\sigma\) curves at ≥3 \(\sigma\) points, (ii) a quadratic fit with a residual plot and goodness-of-fit metrics, and (iii) a Monte-Carlo propagated band over the regulator envelope. The generated artefacts are stored as `outputs/nonlinearity_report.csv` (min/median/max, `nonlinear_flag`), `plots/deltaO_vs_sigma_<EOS>.png`, `plots/quadratic_fit_residuals_<EOS>.png`, and `plots/mc_hist_<EOS>.png`, produced by `scripts/fit_sensitivities.py` and `scripts/sensitivity_nonlinear.py`.


The linear propagation model δO_match ≈ S_O · δσ_match is only reliable in a small-response regime. For observables that can respond nonlinearly (especially Λ), I therefore:


- compute sensitivities at **multiple spans** Δσ ∈ {0.02, 0.04, 0.06} and report **median/min/max** spread; and

- run a **quadratic fit** (and optional Monte‑Carlo sampling of regulator scale) when the spread indicates nonlinearity.


The scripts that produce these artefacts are included (`scripts/fit_sensitivities.py`, `scripts/sensitivity_nonlinear.py`) and write reviewer-facing tables such as `outputs/sensitivities_with_errors.csv` and `outputs/nonlinearity_report.csv` when executed.

A11c. Insertion variants: physical meaning, motivation, and limits

I implement two clearly separated insertion hypotheses to prevent “hidden” model drift:


**Variant A (default; paper-conform): inertial-only insertion.** The vacuum-induced term m_vac≡σχ is treated as an EFT response coefficient that modifies only the inertial (reactive) response entering the (ε+P)-factor in the hydrostatic balance. The gravitational source dm/dr remains sourced by the base EOS energy density ε, i.e. Standard-Model rest-mass content plus EOS internal energy.


**Variant B (exploratory): inertial + gravitational-source insertion.** In addition to the inertial modification, the same vacuum-induced correction is also added to the gravitational source term (dm/dr). This is a stronger hypothesis and may risk conceptual double counting if interpreted as rest-mass; therefore it is not used for headline claims and is provided only as a sensitivity check.


Reviewer-facing rationale: Variant A matches Appendix O.15’s separation (m_bare is SM rest mass; m_vac is an additional EFT response coefficient). Variant B is included solely to test whether qualitative conclusions (sign and order of magnitude of ΔM_max, ΔR_1.4, ΔΛ_1.4) depend on the choice.

A11e. Regulator scan coverage and uncertainty propagation

I report explicit scheme dependence as a systematic uncertainty band. Because Σ_R''(0) depends logarithmically on the UV regulator scale, a single-decade scan can underrepresent worst-case scheme sensitivity. I therefore extend the PV scan by an additional decade (M up to 1000 in model units) and the DimReg scale scan to μ∈[0.1,10]. The resulting envelope defines δσ_match.


Uncertainty propagation (linearized):

δO_match ≈ S_O · δσ_match,

and total uncertainty is reported as a conservative quadrature

δO_total = sqrt(δO_discretization^2 + δO_match^2 + δO_stat^2),

where δO_discretization is estimated from grid-ladder convergence and δO_stat reflects any sampling/statistical uncertainty (if applicable).

1. Physical interpretation and plausible ranges of σ and χ

Within the SFST/QFIS EFT, χ is the static susceptibility of the vacuum saturation field Φ in the IR limit, defined operationally by Φ(ω→0)=χ·E_ext for a chosen low-frequency external source E_ext. In the discrete implementation (Appendix O.3), the inserted inertial contribution per numerical cell is m_vac,cell(r)=σ·χ·S(r)·V_cell, where S(r) is a screening/local factor and V_cell the cell volume.

Appendix O.7 — Fully worked 1-loop example for ΣR''(0) (reproducible PV + DimReg)


Purpose: provide at least one explicit, reproducible 1-loop calculation of the low-frequency coefficient ΣR''(0) in a minimal model, including a concrete regulator choice and numerical evaluation. This serves as an example/micro-check for the controlled matching protocol used to motivate m_vac ∝ ΣR''(0) and thus m0 = m_bare + σ χ.


Minimal model: a slow collective coordinate x(t) couples locally to a quantized scalar vacuum field φ(t,x) via L_int = g x(t) φ(t,0). In linear response, integrating out φ yields an effective kernel K_R(ω) whose ω² term defines an induced mass contribution.


Retarded 1-loop self-energy (standard representation, after analytic continuation):


ΣR(ω) = g² ∫ d³k/(2π)³ [ 1/(2 ω_k) ( 1/(ω − ω_k + i0⁺) − 1/(ω + ω_k + i0⁺) ) ], with ω_k = √(k² + m_φ²).


Taking two derivatives and setting ω=0 gives (formal step):


ΣR''(0) = g² ∫ d³k/(2π)³ 1/ω_k³ .


The integral is UV divergent and must be regularized. Below I give two explicit examples: Pauli–Villars (PV) and dimensional regularization (DimReg).



A) Pauli–Villars example (PV)


Define the PV-regulated integral by subtracting a heavy regulator mass M ≫ m_φ:


I_PV(M) = (1/2π²) ∫₀^∞ dk k² [ (k²+m_φ²)^(-3/2) − (k²+M²)^(-3/2) ].


Then ΣR''(0)_PV = g² I_PV(M). For large M the leading scaling is logarithmic: I_PV(M) ~ (1/2π²) ln(M/m_φ) + O(m_φ²/M²).

Appendix O.10 — Justification of numerical acceptance thresholds


Residual threshold (≤1e−8): chosen so that Newton residuals fall well below truncation error of the second-order spatial discretization for the reported grids; in practice this keeps solver error subdominant to discretization error and ensures percent-level physical effects are not contaminated by solver non-convergence.


CFL factor (≤0.5): standard conservative choice for explicit/semiexplicit stability in the time-dependent 1D reference tests; used to ensure no superluminal or unstable modes appear in the hyperbolic sector when scanning parameters.


Physics acceptance bands (ΔM, ΔR, ΔΛ): anchored to the paper’s documented benchmark effects (≈6%, ≈2%, 10–12%) and widened to accommodate EOS dependence, screening models, and discretization error; the acceptance window is defined to be tighter than the signal size while still allowing known model-to-model variability.


WFaktor stability (<20%): selected as a practical convergence indicator for the diagnostic quantity W under grid refinement and boundary-condition variation; a tighter bound can be enforced once the final benchmark suite is fixed.
